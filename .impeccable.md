# Project: The Scarcity of Novelty

A standalone one-page website hosting a single long-form essay by Igor Zalutski. The essay argues that as AI-generated content becomes abundant, *proof of human authorship* becomes the scarce — and therefore valuable — thing. The site itself is meant to embody that argument: it should feel hand-set, considered, and unmistakably crafted, not templated.

Deployment: GitHub Pages from `opencomputerhq/scarcityofnovelty`. No custom domain.

## Design Context

### Users
Readers of long-form writing — the kind of person who finishes a Paul Graham essay, an Aeon piece, or a Stripe Press chapter. They arrive from a link in a thoughtful person's social feed, expecting one thing: a quiet place to read 1,500 words for ten minutes. They are reading on a laptop or large phone, often at night, often with mild skepticism that whatever they're about to read was actually written by a human.

### Brand Personality
**Bookish · contemplative · unembarrassed.**
The voice is a working founder thinking out loud — first-person, willing to admit "this isn't fully formed yet," lowercase "tbh" / "btw" / "idk" mixed into otherwise careful prose. The page should *honor* that voice: never feel slick, never feel like a landing page, never feel templated. Closer to a printed book than a website. Closer to Kraftwerk's restraint than to a SaaS hero section.

The page must feel like it was set by hand. If a reader wonders "did a person make this?" the answer should be obvious within two seconds.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Literary / bookish, leaning into the conventions of a fine printed essay.**

References:
- The interior pages of a Stripe Press hardback
- An old printed pamphlet with a drop cap and asterism section breaks
- Robin Sloan's email-essay aesthetic
- A Penguin Modern Classics chapter opener

Anti-references:
- AI landing-page slop (gradient-text headlines, glassmorphism, hero metrics, card grids)
- Medium / Substack default theme
- Modern startup minimalism (Inter, neutral grays, "thoughtful" emoji bullets)
- The typical "Notion page made public" look
- Anything that feels "designy" before it feels "readable"

Theme: **light by default**, with a parchment-warm dark mode for late-night readers. The default is light because the bookish referent is a printed page, and people don't read books in the dark.

### Design Principles

1. **The page is a frame for the prose.** Every element either serves the reading experience or it gets cut. Decoration must earn its place by reinforcing the literary mood, not by being clever.

2. **Old typographic conventions, applied seriously.** Drop cap, hanging punctuation, old-style numerals, small caps for the byline, asterism between sections, em dashes (not hyphens). These are not retro affectations — they are how careful typesetters signal care.

3. **Restrained color, warm neutrals.** Cream paper, warm dark ink, a single muted brick-red accent reserved for the drop cap and link hover. No grays. No pure white. No gradients of any kind.

4. **Generous reading measure with comfortable air.** ~40rem column, 1.6 leading, real space between paragraphs. The essay is the only thing on the page; let it breathe.

5. **Proof of craft is the deliverable.** The closing "Written by a human." link to a screen recording is not a footer easter egg — it is the point of the page made literal. Treat it as body text, not a styled signature.
